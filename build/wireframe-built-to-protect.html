Reusable Patterns

These are the structural decisions that survived every round of revision on Built to Protect, through the enlargeable-panels, 6-card, and language-pass rounds. Reapply them to Speaking Through the Dead — but re-derive the specifics (which frameworks, which colors, how many registers/phases) from that exhibit's own content. Don't force a fit.

Pattern 1 (revised)

Exhibit frame vs. commentary — structurally separate, but commentary is now plain, not decorated. The exhibit frame still gets a solid border and an "Exhibit, as installed" tag, and now typically holds two enlargeable images side by side (an installation photo + a panel scan, or two panel scans) rather than one photo plus a rendered text block. Click-to-enlarge via a CSS-only :target lightbox — no JS. Commentary now sits right after the frame as plain flowing text, no "My notes" tag, no dashed indent, no italics — the framed box alone is enough separation. Simpler reads as more confident than over-decorated.

Pattern 2

Framework color key, established once, reused everywhere. Name the relevant frameworks early ("Big Idea & Research Foundation") as color-coded cards. Every pedagogy card later reuses those exact colors. For Built to Protect: brass = UDL ember = OBL sage = EDGE. Speaking Through the Dead needs its own key — its reflection paper only explicitly cites UDL, so reuse brass for that; don't invent an OBL/EDGE equivalent that isn't really there.

Pattern 3 (revised)

One deep example per register/phase — but paired with a thumbnail strip of the rest, not just a sentence. Built to Protect features exactly one station/panel-pair per register in full, then a labeled strip-label ("Other kid stations — same logic, not re-argued in full") followed by 3 real thumbnails, each enlargeable, each with a title + one-line description, not just a bare caption. This replaces the earlier, thinner pattern of "one sentence naming the rest." Coverage without diluting the argument.

Pattern 4 (revised)

Real image over HTML-retyped text — and now the full real text moves into the alt attribute, not onto the visible page. The visible frame shows only the image(s), enlargeable; nothing about them is paraphrased or re-typed as visible copy. Full verbatim label/panel text (including any printed questions) lives in a complete, real alt string, serving accessibility without cluttering the visual design. For Speaking Through the Dead, the 16 screenshots are the highest-res source available — crop/use directly, and transcribe their on-screen text into alt attributes.

Pattern 5

Confident, achievement-framed conclusion. No "still not finished" hedging. End by tying the frameworks together explicitly and naming what was proven. This is a portfolio, not a reflection paper.

Pattern 6

The design-decision section stays short — two tight paragraphs, evidence-based. State the ambitious original version, the test/evidence, the finding, the change. Don't narrate the whole process.

Pattern 7

Register/phase headers are real, visible, colored typography — display-serif size, colored to the dominant framework where one applies cleanly. Don't force a two-register split onto content that has one audience, not two (see Speaking Through the Dead's "Narrative / Interactives" split by means, not audience).

Pattern 8

Bigger type than feels necessary at first pass. Root font-size increased; smallest mono/label text bumped further individually.

Pattern 9 (new)

Pedagogy callouts are substantial explanatory cards, six per deep example, laid out 3 wide × 2 rows. Each card: a bolded concept name, a one-sentence definition of the concept (quoting the actual research-brief language where it exists), then a sentence connecting it specifically to what's on the wall. Six is enough to search out genuinely distinct connections (physical mounting height, reading level, voice, collaboration mode, aesthetic register) rather than repeating "here's another EDGE attribute" three times with no new observation. When two deep examples exist (kid + adult), look for at least one card in each that contrasts or explicitly parallels the other (e.g. "Object at Eye Level" for kids vs. "Object at Eye Level, Recalibrated" for adults) — this proves the design decisions were deliberate at every station, not incidental.

Pattern 10 (new)

Language pass, every round: active first-person voice ("I printed one tree full-size") over passive constructions ("was tested"); real quoted source language over paraphrase, wherever a source document has the words already; no fabricated fake-precise details (don't invent "four seconds" or a stat that wasn't measured); don't reuse the same rhetorical construction more than once on a page (watch for repeated "not X, just Y" antithesis, or one favorite word like "calibrated" used three different ways). Read the page aloud, listen for anything that sounds like a template filled in six times.

Pattern 11 (new)

Thumbnails get a title AND a one-line description, not just a bare caption. E.g. "The Helmet / Two helmets, a century apart — same open questions," not just "The Helmet."

Pattern 12 (new)

Enlargeable images use a CSS-only :target lightbox. Each enlargeable image appears twice in the HTML source (inline + lightbox target) since there's no JS to reuse a single reference — this roughly doubles that image's embedded weight in the current base64-artifact-preview stage. Acceptable for now; disappears once this is a real GitHub Pages site with actual image files the browser can reference once and cache.

2 — The Big Idea & Research Foundation
Commentary — active voice, "I grounded..."

"Engineering is never finished" / "Safety Isn't Solved" — one idea, two reading levels, tied to the concept doc's own line about "mental models... that apply far beyond firefighting gear." Before drafting a label, I grounded the design in three frameworks — the color key, established once:

UDL Multiple means of representation, engagement & expression
OBL Lead with the object, layer the labels, let objects generate the question
EDGE Exploratorium's 9 attributes for engaging girls in STEM without alienating boys

3 — The Prototype Test (half-length — 2 tight paragraphs, active voice)
Commentary

I originally designed four stations, flip-panel "decision trees," real historical forks, three real paths each, a reveal. Ambitious, grounded in research.

Testing said otherwise. I printed one tree full-size and watched families use it — noting where they paused, what they skipped, what got a parent and kid talking. Reading fatigue by this point in a visit. What worked was handling objects and talking to each other, not solving a text puzzle. I changed the mechanism, kept the Big Idea.
