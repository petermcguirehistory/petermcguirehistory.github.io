My path to museums

Sixteen years of professional writing taught me to know who's reading and write straight to them: five-hundred-page technical manuals for engineers, candy ads for brands like Starburst, content-marketing blogs. Each needed a different voice.

In 2019, I started Unlikely Explanations to bring those skills to my real passion: history. I found original stories and followed them—pieces like "Why Did Steamboat Springs Blow Up The Steamboat Spring?," which used railroad history to explore differing Ute and white-settler attitudes toward the land itself, and "How Do We Cope With Gender?," which traced eunuchs, proto-trans figures, and third genders across history. Publishing these stories taught me about the fraught transmission and reception of history firsthand.

Through the interviews I did for those stories—including one with Dr. Michael Barkun about his book A Culture of Conspiracy—I kept running into the same idea: that history can act as a kind of inoculation against radicalization and bigotry by creating spaces for exploring complexity, away from the false certainty of political broadcasting. As I have learned about education through my MA, my respect for informal learning and adult education has only grown.

Built to Protect and Speaking Through the Dead, on the Exhibits page, bring my writing career together with my new Museum Studies skills. I think people learn best when they explore and reach their own conclusions. I built these kinds of experiences for brands to drive web visitors to a point of purchase. Now, I can apply the same skills to the more noble pursuit of education.

The social-network graph and flow diagram above are two of the interactives I built for Speaking Through the Dead. I learned to build sites and visualizations like these as a content creator, long before I called it public history.

Let's bring history to life together.
