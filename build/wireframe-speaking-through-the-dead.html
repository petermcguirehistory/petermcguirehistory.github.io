Bridge, not a third deep example — the four mechanisms (Stop Two) that connect this stop to the exclusion argument below, in his own analytical terms:

  • The parlor's private network went public — Amy Post's world became the livelihood of female traveling lecturers
  • Channeling gave women a socially acceptable public voice, bypassing male gatekeepers
  • Spiritualism handed women practical control over piety, long controlled by patriarchal theology
  • It let women appropriate authority from outside sources — the same move that turns, next stop, into appropriating Native and Asian voices

Plain commentary: "Reusing the same tool with re-contextualized framing turns the historiographical argument into something a visitor watches happen, not just reads — the network redraws itself, and Amy Post visibly moves from center to edge, while Elizabeth Cady Stanton — who literally authored the movement's official history — moves to the center. It's a visual answer to a question Audre Lorde raised: does winning a right through respectability amount to real change, or just success on someone else's terms? Erasing Spiritualism from feminist history isn't a neutral editing choice — it's the same kind of erasure Lorde pointed to decades later, when women of color were still treated as outsiders to a movement they'd helped build."
